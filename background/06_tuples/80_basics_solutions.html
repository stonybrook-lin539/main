Tuples: The basics (Solutions)

For each one of the following tuples, compute its length.

  1. \(\left \langle a,b,a \right \rangle\)
  2. \(\left \langle a,\left \langle b \right \rangle,a \right \rangle\)
  3. \(\left \langle a,\left \langle b,a \right \rangle \right \rangle\)
  4. \(\left \langle \left \{ a,b \right \}, \left \langle a \right \rangle \right \rangle\)
  5. \(\left \langle \left \langle a, b,\left \{ a,a \right \} \right \rangle \right \rangle\)
  1. \(3\)
  2. \(3\)
  3. \(2\)
  4. \(2\)
  5. \(1\)
  1. This tuple has three components, which are \(a\), \(b\), and \(a\) again. Keep in mind that in contrast to set cardinality, duplicates are not ignored when computing the length of a tuple.
  2. The second component has changed from \(b\) to \(\left \langle b \right \rangle\), but this does not change how many components there are.
  3. This tuple has two components, the first being \(a\), the second being the tuple \(\left \langle b,a \right \rangle\). Note that we do not consider the internal make-up of that tuple when calculating the number of components of the containing tuple.
  4. Again we have two components. The first one is the set \(\left \{ a,b \right \}\) (and again we ignore the internal make-up of the set), and other is \(\left \langle a \right \rangle\).
  5. This tuple contains only one component, which is \(\left \langle a, b, \left \{ a,a \right \} \right \rangle\). Again it does not matter that this tuple has multiple components.

For each one of the formulas below, what is its value?

  1. \(\pi_3(\left \langle a,b,c,d \right \rangle)\)
  2. \(\pi_8(\left \langle a,b,c,d \right \rangle)\)
  3. \(\pi_0(\left \langle a,b,c,d \right \rangle)\)
  4. \(\pi_3(\left \langle a,b,\left \langle c,d \right \rangle \right \rangle)\)
  5. \(\pi_1(\pi_3(\left \langle a,b,\left \langle c,d \right \rangle \right \rangle))\)
  1. \(c\)
  2. undefined
  3. undefined
  4. \(\left \langle c,d \right \rangle\)
  5. \(c\)
  1. \(\pi_3\) returns the third component of its argument \(\left \langle a,b,c,d \right \rangle\), which is \(c\).
  2. \(\pi_8\) is supposed to return the 8th component of its argument, but \(\left \langle a,b,c,d \right \rangle\) only has 4 components.
  3. Similarly, there is no such thing as the 0th component of a tuple.
  4. Nothing special going on here, we simply determine the third component. It does not matter at this point that this component happens to be a tuple.
  5. As we just saw, \(\pi_3(\left \langle a,b,\left \langle c,d \right \rangle \right \rangle)\) is \(\left \langle c,d \right \rangle\). This becomes the argument of \(\pi_1\), which returns its first component, which is \(c\).

Explain why \(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle = \left \langle b,a \right \rangle\) implies \(a = b\).

The only way two tuples can be identical is if all their respective components are identical. Hence \(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle = \left \langle b,a \right \rangle\) holds only if the first components of these tuples are identical and the second components of the tuples are identical. Hence \(\pi_1(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle) = \pi_1(\left \langle b,a \right \rangle)\), which is the same as saying \(a = b\). We get the same result if we use \(\pi_2\) instead of \(\pi_1\).

Fill in the gaps with \(=\) and \(\neq\) as appropriate, assuming that \(a\) and \(b\) are distinct elements:

  1. \(\left \langle a \right \rangle \_ \left \langle b \right \rangle\)
  2. \(\left \langle a,b,a \right \rangle \_ \left \langle a,b,b \right \rangle\)
  3. \(\left \langle a,b,a,a \right \rangle \_ \left \langle a,b,a,a \right \rangle\)
  4. \(\left \langle a,a,b,a \right \rangle \_ \left \langle a,b,a,a \right \rangle\)
  5. \(\left \langle a,a,b,a \right \rangle \_ \left \langle a,a,\left \{ b \right \},a \right \rangle\)
  6. \(\left \langle a,a,b,a \right \rangle \_ \left \langle a,\left \langle a,b \right \rangle,a \right \rangle\)
  1. \(\left \langle a \right \rangle \neq \left \langle b \right \rangle\)
  2. \(\left \langle a,b,a \right \rangle \neq \left \langle a,b,b \right \rangle\)
  3. \(\left \langle a,b,a,a \right \rangle = \left \langle a,b,a,a \right \rangle\)
  4. \(\left \langle a,a,b,a \right \rangle \neq \left \langle a,b,a,a \right \rangle\)
  5. \(\left \langle a,a,b,a \right \rangle \neq \left \langle a,a,\left \{ b \right \},a \right \rangle\)
  6. \(\left \langle a,a,b,a \right \rangle \neq \left \langle a,\left \langle a,b \right \rangle,a \right \rangle\)

Two \(n\)-tuples are identical iff it holds for every \(i\) between 1 and \(n\) that the \(i\)-th components of the two tuples are the same.

  1. \(\left \langle a \right \rangle \neq \left \langle b \right \rangle\): Since \(a \neq b\), the initial components of these 1-tuples are distinct, and thus the tuples are distinct.
  2. \(\left \langle a,b,a \right \rangle \neq \left \langle a,b,b \right \rangle\): Again we have \(a \neq b\), but this time this matters for the third component, where the first tuple has \(a\) and the second tuple has \(b\).
  3. \(\left \langle a,b,a,a \right \rangle = \left \langle a,b,a,a \right \rangle\): These tuples have exactly the same components. Going though the components from left to right, we have \(a = a\), \(b = b\), \(a = a\), and \(a = a\).
  4. \(\left \langle a,a,b,a \right \rangle \neq \left \langle a,b,a,a \right \rangle\): Here the tuples disagree on their second component as well as on their third component.
  5. \(\left \langle a,a,b,a \right \rangle \neq \left \langle a,a,\left \{ b \right \},a \right \rangle\): Remember that \(b \neq \left \{ b \right \}\), so these tuples disagree on their third component.
  6. \(\left \langle a,a,b,a \right \rangle \neq \left \langle a,\left \langle a,b \right \rangle,a \right \rangle\): The first tuple is a 4-tuple with the components \(a\), \(a\), \(b\), and \(a\). The second tuple is a triple with the components \(a\), \(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle\), and \(a\).

Calculate the result of the following concatenations:

  1. \(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot((\left \langle c \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle a,b \right \rangle) \cdot\left \langle a,c,e \right \rangle)\)
  2. \((\left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle c \right \rangle) \cdot(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle a,c,e \right \rangle)\)
  1. \(\left \langle a,b,c,a,b,a,c,e \right \rangle\)

All we have to do is to resolve the concatenations step by step. $ \[\begin{align*} \left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot((\left \langle c \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle a,b \right \rangle) \cdot\left \langle a,c,e \right \rangle) &= \left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot(\left \langle c,a,b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle a,c,e \right \rangle)\\ &= \left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot(\left \langle c,a,b,a,c,e \right \rangle\\ &= \left \langle a,b,c,a,b,a,c,e \right \rangle\\ \end{align*}\] $

$ \[\begin{align*} (\left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle c \right \rangle) \cdot(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle a,c,e \right \rangle) &= \left \langle a,b,c \right \rangle \cdot(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle a,c,e \right \rangle)\\ &= \left \langle a,b,c \right \rangle \cdot(\left \langle a,b,a,c,e \right \rangle)\\ &= \left \langle a,b,c,a,b,a,c,e \right \rangle\\ \end{align*}\] $

Write down all possible ways of obtaining \(\left \langle a,b,c,d \right \rangle\) via concatenation of tuples whose length is at least \(1\). For instance, one possible way is \(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle c, d \right \rangle\), but there’s several more.

Since concatenation is associative, we can ignore bracketing. This leaves us with the following 7 options:

  1. \(\left \langle a \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle c \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle d \right \rangle\)
  2. \(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle c \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle d \right \rangle\)
  3. \(\left \langle a \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle b,c \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle d \right \rangle\)
  4. \(\left \langle a \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle c,d \right \rangle\)
  5. \(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle c,d \right \rangle\)
  6. \(\left \langle a \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle b,c,d \right \rangle\)
  7. \(\left \langle a,b,c \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle d \right \rangle\)

Tuple concatenation is not commutative. That is to say, for some tuples \(a\) and \(b\) it is not the case that \(a \cdot b = b \cdot a\). Illustrate this with an example.

The simplest example is \(a = \left \langle 0 \right \rangle\) and \(b = \left \langle 1 \right \rangle\). Then \(a \cdot b = \left \langle 0 \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle 1 \right \rangle = \left \langle 0,1 \right \rangle \neq \left \langle 1,0 \right \rangle = \left \langle 1 \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle 0 \right \rangle = b \cdot a\).

The solutions is fairly self-explanatory, but one additional remark may be helpful here. With strings we saw that there can be cases where \(a \cdot b = b \cdot a\) even though \(a \neq b\). For instance, if \(a\) is the string ba and \(b\) is the string baba, then \(a \neq b\) yet \(a \cdot b\) and \(b \cdot a\) are both bababa. With tuples, the situation is not as clear cut. If \(a \mathrel{\mathop:}=\left \langle b,a \right \rangle\) and \(b \mathrel{\mathop:}=\left \langle b,a,b,a \right \rangle\), then \(a \cdot b = \left \langle b,a,b,a,b,a \right \rangle = b \cdot a\). But if \(a \mathrel{\mathop:}=\left \langle ba \right \rangle\) and \(b \mathrel{\mathop:}=\left \langle baba \right \rangle\), then \(a \cdot b = \left \langle ba, baba \right \rangle \neq \left \langle baba, ba \right \rangle = b \cdot a\).

Compute \(\left \langle a \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle \right \rangle\) and \(\left \langle \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle a,a,a \right \rangle\).

  1. \(\left \langle a \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle \right \rangle = \left \langle a \right \rangle\)
  2. \(\left \langle \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle a,a,a \right \rangle = \left \langle a,a,a \right \rangle\).

Does the concatenation of \(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle\) and \(\left \langle c \right \rangle\) yield the same result as the concatenation of \(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle\) and \(\left \langle \left \langle c \right \rangle \right \rangle\)? Justify your answer!

No. We have \(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle c \right \rangle = \left \langle a,b,c \right \rangle\), whereas \(\left \langle a,b \right \rangle \cdot\left \langle \left \langle c \right \rangle \right \rangle = \left \langle a,b,\left \langle c \right \rangle \right \rangle\).
