This exercise is only for those who have already delved deep into the background sections: Explain why \(\triangleleft\) is a weak partial order.

Hint: Remember the three properties that each weak partial order must satisfy, and explain for each one why it holds.

The relation \(\triangleleft\) is a weak partial order because it is transitive, antisymmetric, and reflexive. In the following, I write \(m \prec=n\) as a shorthand for “\(m \prec n\) or \(m = n\)”.

  1. Transitivity: Suppose \(\left \langle a,x \right \rangle \triangleleft \left \langle b,y \right \rangle\) and \(\left \langle b,y \right \rangle \triangleleft \left \langle c,z \right \rangle\). This entails that \(a \leq b \leq c\) and \(x \prec= y \prec= z\). But then, by the definition of \(\leq\) and \(\prec=\), it holds that \(\left \langle a,x \right \rangle \triangleleft \left \langle b,y \right \rangle\).

  2. Antisymmetry: Suppose \(\left \langle a,x \right \rangle \triangleleft \left \langle b,y \right \rangle\) and \(\left \langle b,y \right \rangle \triangleleft \left \langle a,x \right \rangle\). This entails that \(a \leq b\), \(b \leq a\), \(x \prec= y\), and \(y \prec= x\). But then, by the definition of \(\leq\) and \(\prec=\), it holds that \(a = b\) and \(x = y\), so that \(\left \langle a,x \right \rangle = \left \langle b,y \right \rangle\).

  3. Reflexivity: By definition \(\left \langle a,x \right \rangle \triangleleft \left \langle b,y \right \rangle\) iff \(a \leq b\) or \(x \prec= y\). This holds if \(a = b\) and \(x = y\), so that \(\left \langle a,x \right \rangle \triangleleft \left \langle a,x \right \rangle\) for all choices of \(a\) and \(x\).
